<dd class="glossary">The dynamic set of economic and technological factors that affect <a href="#agriculture"><span title="See entry on this page at § agriculture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">agricultural</span></a> practices in a particular region.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="agrarianism" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarianism</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">A social or political philosophy which values rural society as superior to urban society and the independent farmer as superior to the paid worker. Agrarianism argues in favor of <a href="#farming"><span title="See entry on this page at § farming" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">farming</span></a> as a way of life that can shape ideal social values.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="agribusiness" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Agribusiness" title="Agribusiness">agribusiness</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The business of agricultural production, including the entire range of activities and disciplines encompassed by modern food and fiber production chains and those agents and institutions which influence them.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="agricultural_cooperative" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Agricultural_cooperative" title="Agricultural cooperative">agricultural cooperative</a></dfn></dt>
<p class="glossary-hatnote"><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">Also called a <b>farmers' co-op</b> or simply a <b>co-op</b>.</span></p>
<dd class="glossary">Any association of farmers or agricultural businesses who voluntarily pool their resources in order to meet their common agricultural needs and goals by cooperating in a jointly owned enterprise. Agricultural cooperatives may be distinguished between "service" cooperatives, which provide inputs for agricultural production (seeds, fertilizers, fuels, etc.) or transportation and marketing services to members who run their farms individually, and "production" cooperatives, in which members run their farms jointly using shared land, machinery, or other resources; an example of the latter is <a href="#collective_farming"><span title="See entry on this page at § collective farming" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">collective farming</span></a>.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="agricultural_economics" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Agricultural_economics" title="Agricultural economics">agricultural economics</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The branch of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> concerned with the application of economic theory in optimizing the production and distribution of food, fiber, and other products of agriculture.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="agricultural_land" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Agricultural_land" title="Agricultural land">agricultural land</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Any land devoted solely to <a href="#agriculture"><span title="See entry on this page at § agriculture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">agriculture</span></a>, i.e. the deliberate and systematic reproduction of living organisms in order to produce commodities that can be used by humans. In the broadest sense, agricultural land may also include certain types of land which are used only partially or seasonally for agricultural purposes, such as <a href="#pasture"><span title="See entry on this page at § pasture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">pastures</span></a> and wild forests. Colloquially, the term is often used interchangeably with <a href="#farmland"><span title="See entry on this page at § farmland" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">farmland</span></a>, <a href="#cropland"><span title="See entry on this page at § cropland" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">cropland</span></a>, and <a href="#arable_land"><span title="See entry on this page at § arable land" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">arable land</span></a>, though these terms may also be considered technically distinct.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="agricultural_productivity" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Agricultural_productivity" title="Agricultural productivity">agricultural productivity</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">A measure of the economic <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a> of a given quantity of <a href="#agricultural_land"><span title="See entry on this page at § agricultural land" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">agricultural land</span></a> (or any other agricultural input), typically expressed as the ratio of agricultural outputs to agricultural inputs. In modern agricultural industries, "output" is often quantified as the <a href="/wiki/Market_value" title="Market value">market value</a> of the agricultural product at the end of the production chain (i.e. immediately prior to its purchase by a consumer).</dd>

The term is often used interchangeably with <a href="#agronomy"><span title="See entry on this page at § agronomy" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">agronomy</span></a>, <a href="#agricultural_science"><span title="See entry on this page at § agricultural science" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">agricultural science</span></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_soil_science" title="Agricultural soil science">agricultural soil science</a>.</dd>

<p class="glossary-hatnote"><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">Also called <b>beekeeping</b>.</span></p>
<dd class="glossary">The maintenance of colonies of <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>, commonly in man-made <a href="/wiki/Beehive" title="Beehive">beehives</a>, by humans for any of a variety of purposes, including collecting <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> or other products created by bees, pollinating crops, and breeding bees for sale. A location where bees are kept is called an <i><a href="/wiki/Apiary" title="Apiary">apiary</a></i> and a person who practices apiculture is called an <i>apiarist</i> or <i>beekeeper</i>.</dd>

Branches of aquaculture include <a href="#pisciculture"><span title="See entry on this page at § pisciculture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">pisciculture</span></a>, <a href="#algaculture"><span title="See entry on this page at § algaculture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">algaculture</span></a>, and <a href="#mariculture"><span title="See entry on this page at § mariculture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">mariculture</span></a>.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="artificial_selection" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">artificial selection</a></dfn></dt>
<p class="glossary-hatnote"><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">Also called <b>selective breeding</b>.</span></p>
<dd class="glossary">The process by which humans use <a href="/wiki/Animal_breeding" title="Animal breeding">animal breeding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">plant breeding</a> to <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">selectively</a> control the development of particular <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypic traits</a> in organisms by choosing which individual organisms will reproduce and create <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a>. Artificial selection involves the deliberate exploitation of knowledge about <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_biology" title="Reproductive biology">reproductive biology</a> in the hope of producing desirable characteristics in descendant organisms. It is widely practiced in <a href="#agriculture"><span title="See entry on this page at § agriculture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">agriculture</span></a>, but it may also be unintentional and may produce unintended results.</dd>

<dd class="glossary">A type of alternative agriculture which incorporates holistic ecological approaches and aspects of <a href="#organic_farming"><span title="See entry on this page at § organic farming" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">organic</span></a> and <a href="#integrated_farming"><span title="See entry on this page at § integrated farming" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">integrated farming</span></a> but also emphasizes various esoteric perspectives, including spiritual and mystical beliefs about nature. The efficacy of biodynamic agricultural techniques lacks scientific evidence, and the practice has been labeled a <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscience</a>.</dd>

In a looser sense it may also include <a href="#fodder"><span title="See entry on this page at § fodder" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">fodder</span></a> (plant material deliberately cut and given to animals as food).</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="furrow_irrigation" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Furrow_irrigation" class="mw-redirect" title="Furrow irrigation">furrow irrigation</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">A type of <a href="#irrigation"><span title="See entry on this page at § irrigation" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">irrigation</span></a> which relies on long, shallow, parallel channels, known as <i>furrows</i>, dug into the soil along the length of an agricultural field to deliver water to crops planted on the ridges between the furrows. Furrow irrigation is particularly suited to broadacre <a href="#row_crop"><span title="See entry on this page at § row crop" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">row crops</span></a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="growing_season" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The part of the year during which local weather conditions (i.e. temperature and precipitation) permit the normal growth of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> in a given location. Though the timing of plant growth and reproduction can vary widely by species, many local plant species show considerable <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenological</a> overlap, and so the term is commonly used to refer to a single generic season that encompasses a majority of the plants or crops growing in a given location. In many places, the local "growing season" is defined as the period of time between the average date of the last <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">frost</a> (in temperate parts of the <a href="#northern_Hemisphere"><span title="See entry on this page at § Northern Hemisphere" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">Northern Hemisphere</span></a>, this typically occurs in the spring or early summer) and the average date of the first frost (typically in the autumn).</dd>

<dd class="glossary">Any method of growing <a href="#crop"><span title="See entry on this page at § crop" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">crops</span></a> or maintaining <a href="#pasture"><span title="See entry on this page at § pasture" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">pasture</span></a> without disturbing the soil through <a href="#tillage"><span title="See entry on this page at § tillage" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">tillage</span></a>. <dt class="glossary" id="staple_food" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staple food</a></dfn></dt>
<p class="glossary-hatnote"><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">Also simply <b>staple</b>.</span></p>
<dd class="glossary">A food that is eaten routinely and in such quantities that it constitutes a dominant portion of a standard diet for a given population or demographic, supplying a large fraction of energy needs and generally providing a significant proportion of the basic nutrients needed for survival or health. Specific staple foods vary by location and culture, but typically are inexpensive or readily available foods that are suitable for storage over long periods of time without decay; examples include <a href="#cereal"><span title="See entry on this page at § cereal" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">cereals</span></a>, starchy <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Root_vegetable" class="mw-redirect" title="Root vegetable">root vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish_as_food" title="Fish as food">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egg_as_food" title="Egg as food">eggs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dairy_product" title="Dairy product">dairy products</a>.</dd>

<dd class="glossary">The practice of leaving the stubble or <a href="#crop_residue"><span title="See entry on this page at § crop residue" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">crop residue</span></a> essentially in place on a plot of cropland as a surface cover during a <a href="#fallow"><span title="See entry on this page at § fallow" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">fallow</span></a> period. Stubble-mulching can prevent soil erosion and conserve soil moisture.<sup id="cite_ref-CRS_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="threshing" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Threshing" title="Threshing">threshing</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The process of loosening the edible part of a <a href="#grain"><span title="See entry on this page at § grain" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">grain</span></a> or other crop from the <a href="#chaff"><span title="See entry on this page at § chaff" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">chaff</span></a> to which it is attached, without removing the <a href="/wiki/Bran" title="Bran">bran</a>. In grain cultivation, threshing immediately follows <a href="#reaping"><span title="See entry on this page at § reaping" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">reaping</span></a>.</dd>

<dt class="glossary" id="tillage" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tillage</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">1.&#160;&#160;The preparation of agricultural soil by any of various types of mechanical agitation, whether human-powered, animal-powered, or mechanised, such as digging, <a href="/wiki/Hoe_(tool)" title="Hoe (tool)">hoeing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rake_(tool)" title="Rake (tool)">raking</a>, <a href="#plough"><span title="See entry on this page at § plough" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">ploughing</span></a>, and <a href="#harrow"><span title="See entry on this page at § harrow" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">harrowing</span></a>. In this sense, it is also referred to as <i>tilling</i>.</dd>
<dd class="glossary">2.&#160;&#160;The land that is tilled.</dd>

<dd class="glossary">Any plant that is cultivated in order to attract the attention of agricultural pests, usually insects, and thereby distract them away from nearby <a href="#crop"><span title="See entry on this page at § crop" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">crops</span></a>. <dt class="glossary" id="xeriscaping" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary"><a href="/wiki/Xeriscaping" title="Xeriscaping">xeriscaping</a></dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">The practice of gardening or landscaping so as to reduce or eliminate the need for supplemental water from <a href="#irrigation"><span title="See entry on this page at § irrigation" class="glossary-link-internal" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed #86a1ff; color:initial;" id="">irrigation</span></a>. Xeriscaping requires the selection of plants whose natural requirements are appropriate to the local climate, with a particular emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a>, and focuses on designing and maintaining the land in such a way as to avoid losing water to evaporation and run-off.</dd>
